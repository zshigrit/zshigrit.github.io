Teaching

Teaching Philosophy

My teaching integrates quantitative rigor with ecological intuition. I emphasize three principles: connecting the big picture, engaging students through interaction, and building quantitative confidence.

I aim to help students understand how ecological theory, biogeochemistry, and modeling connect within the broader landscape of biological science. In my courses, theoretical concepts are translated into computational form using mathematical models, statistical analysis, and coding exercises. I view teaching as a two-way learning process, where discussion, curiosity, and collaborative exploration are essential.

Future courses

  • Experimental Design and Data Analysis for Biologists
    Introduction to hypothesis testing, ANOVA, multiple comparisons, and application of statistical methods to biological experiments.
  • Ecosystem Modeling and Data Assimilation
    Integration of process-based ecosystem models, Bayesian inference, and data assimilation techniques for terrestrial carbon cycling.
  • Machine learning and its application in ecology
    This course introduces fundamental machine learning concepts and their applications in ecological research. Students will learn core methods such as regression, classification, neural networks, and model evaluation, with emphasis on analyzing ecological data and improving predictive understanding of ecological processes. Through hands-on coding exercises and case studies, the course demonstrates how machine learning can support ecological modeling, forecasting, and data integration.
  • Frontiers in Global Change Biology (Seminar)
    Critical discussion of recent research on climate–biosphere interactions and scientific communication.

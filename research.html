About

The EcoDynamics Lab, led by Zheng Shi, bridges ecosystem ecology, statistics, and process-based modeling to understand how plant–soil systems respond to environmental change. Our work integrates empirical data, mechanistic theory, and machine learning to improve prediction of terrestrial carbon cycling under global change.

Dr. Shi received his Ph.D. in Global Change Ecology from the University of Oklahoma (2015). He previously held postdoctoral positions at Oak Ridge National Laboratory and the University of California, Irvine, and is currently a Research Scientist at the Institute for Environmental Genomics at the University of Oklahoma.

Research Vision

Our long-term goal is to develop data–model integration frameworks that connect microbial processes, soil carbon persistence, and Earth system models. By combining biological statistics and machine learning with process-based modeling, we aim to reduce uncertainty in carbon–climate feedback projections and advance predictive ecology.

Current Funding

Research Themes

1. Field Experiments

Long-term multifactor climate manipulation experiments reveal how plant communities, microbial processes, and soil carbon pools respond to warming, precipitation change, and land management. We quantify mechanisms underlying ecosystem carbon balance using statistical and experimental approaches.

2. Data Synthesis and Upscaling

We synthesize global datasets—including soil radiocarbon measurements, carbon profiles, and microbial traits—and use statistical learning to generate spatially explicit predictions of soil carbon persistence.

3. Mechanistic Modeling

We develop and benchmark terrestrial ecosystem and Earth system models to diagnose structural uncertainties and improve representation of carbon–climate feedback mechanisms.

4. Machine Learning Integration

We design machine-learning-enabled emulators and parameter-learning systems that couple neural networks with process-based models to enhance prediction, interpretability, and mechanistic understanding.

    Research Themes

    Field Experiments

    We quantify ecosystem responses to environmental change by linking plant productivity and soil respiration— the two largest carbon fluxes that jointly determine ecosystem carbon sequestration. Our work leverages long-term field manipulations (e.g., warming, altered precipitation, and land management such as hay harvest) to reveal how plant community composition, microbial activity, and ecosystem carbon cycling co-evolve through time.

    A key emphasis is identifying mechanisms (and their time dependence) that control carbon balance and stability, using rigorous statistical inference (e.g., mixed-effects models, multivariate analysis, and structural approaches) and extending site-level insights to broader, management-relevant understanding through synthesis.

    Data Synthesis

    We integrate multi-source datasets to constrain patterns and drivers of soil carbon storage, turnover, and persistence across space. This includes global syntheses and meta-analyses that test whether ecosystem carbon responses exhibit consistent directions under warming, drought, and precipitation extremes, and when they do not.

    A major focus is using global observational constraints—especially soil radiocarbon datasets—as an anchor for evaluating soil carbon persistence and diagnosing model–data mismatches at scales relevant for policy and Earth system prediction.

    Mechanistic Modeling

    We develop and evaluate process-based models to understand mechanisms and uncertainties in soil carbon responses to climate change. Our modeling work emphasizes structural uncertainty (how different model structures produce divergent projections) and the trade-off between model realism and uncertainty amplification when complexity increases.

    We specifically target processes that govern long-term soil carbon dynamics—turnover time and microbial carbon use efficiency— and use multiple data streams (e.g., radiocarbon, incubations, and profile datasets) to quantify these processes and diagnose why models can overestimate sequestration under global change scenarios.

    Machine Learning

    We use machine learning and deep learning to upscale sparse but information-rich measurements (especially soil radiocarbon) and to identify dominant drivers of soil carbon persistence and storage at global scale. Our ML-based upscaling enables grid-to-grid comparisons with Earth system models, revealing where models overestimate sequestration and where apparent multi-model agreement can mask deeper mechanistic inconsistencies.

    Beyond prediction, we use ML for mechanism discovery—e.g., quantifying the relative influence of carbon use efficiency versus other processes—and to build ML-enabled benchmarking and emulator frameworks that support scalable diagnosis, uncertainty mapping, and model development.

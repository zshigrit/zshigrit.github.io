Research Themes

Ecosystem Responses to Environmental Change: field experiments and data synthesis

Plant productivity and soil respiration are the two largest carbon fluxes into and out of ecosystems, and their balance determines ecosystem carbon sequestration. Climate change and land management practices profoundly affect both fluxes. Using analysis of variance (ANOVA), principal component analysis (PCA), linear mixed-effects model (LMM), structure equation modeling (SEM), and meta-analysis, I have revealed critical mechanisms that determine ecosystem responses to environmental change. For example, in a tallgrass prairie, experimental warming and hay harvest altered plant community composition and diversity (Shi et al. 2015 Journal of Ecology), which in turn promoted plant productivity and enhanced its temporal stability (Shi et al. 2016 Nature Communications). The shifted community also amplified the response of soil respiration to warming over time (Xu and Shi et al. 2015 Global Change Biology). Together, these changes led toward carbon neutrality, where increased productivity was balanced by enhanced soil respiration (Shi et al. 2015 Ecosphere). In another field experiment, I found that plant productivity responded strongly to drought and increased precipitation, but not to warming (Shi et al. 2018 Global Change Biology). Soil carbon stocks remained stable, reflecting the unchanged carbon balance under altered temperature and precipitation (Xu# and Shi# et al. 2016 Global Change Biology; #equal contribution). To extend these experimental insights to management and policy-relevant scales, I conducted global syntheses. A meta-analysis of 83 precipitation experiments revealed that mesic grasslands were more sensitive to decreased precipitation but less sensitive to increased precipitation compared with arid grasslands (Wilcox# and Shi# et al. 2017 Global Change Biology). Another meta-analysis showed that asymmetric responses of productivity and respiration to drought were strongest in grasslands relative to forests and savannas (Shi et al. 2014 Biogeosciences). Meanwhile, our synthesis of global warming experiments demonstrated no consistent evidence for either positive or negative soil carbon sequestration across ecosystems (van Gestel and Shi et al. 2018 Nature).

Soil carbon persistence and dynamics: data-driven modeling and upscaling with machine learning

Another major theme of my research focuses on identifying key processes of soil carbon sequestration using multi-source datasets. The goal is to develop data-driven models and reduce uncertainty in long-term soil carbon predictions. By assimilating global microbial and soil carbon datasets, we found that two complex soil carbon models predicted widely varying magnitudes and directions of global carbon sequestration (Shi et al. 2018 Nature Communications). In contrast, a simpler, data-constrained model predicted small positive sequestration. This work emphasized that diverse model structures increase confidence in projections, but that excessive complexity can also amplify uncertainty. We highlighted the need for balance between model realism and structural diversity, alongside improved global datasets for model constraint. One of the most critical datasets for constraining model predictions is soil radiocarbon abundance, a robust indicator of carbon persistence. We compiled thousands of site-level radiocarbon measurements (Lawrence, …Shi et al. 2020 Earth System Science Data) and upscaled these using ML algorithms to produce global radiocarbon maps (Shi et al. 2020, Nature Geoscience). Our results showed that climate dominates topsoil radiocarbon variability, while soil depth controls deeper carbon age distributions. Compared with Earth System Model (ESM) outputs, the ML-upscaled data revealed that existing models overestimate soil carbon sequestration under global change. Building on this work, I demonstrated that global-scale model convergence across multiple ESMs can mask deep inconsistencies in predicted soil carbon changes (Shi et al. 2024, AGU Advances). Furthermore, using over 70,000 soil carbon profiles and deep learning algorithms, we found that carbon use efficiency is at least four times as influential as carbon input, decomposition, or vertical transport in determining global soil carbon storage (Tao,…Shi et al. 2023 Nature). Together, these studies define a quantitative, data-informed framework for improving how global models represent soil carbon dynamics, persistence, and uncertainty.

Beyond prediction, we use ML for mechanism discovery—e.g., quantifying the relative influence of carbon use efficiency versus other processes—and to build ML-enabled benchmarking and emulator frameworks that support scalable diagnosis, uncertainty mapping, and model development.

Machine Learning–Enabled Model Benchmarking and development

My group will apply ML to detect structural errors and inconsistencies in multi-model ensembles, building on my recent work (Shi et al. 2024 AGU Advances). We will develop deep learning emulators that reproduce the behavior of land carbon models, allowing scalable benchmarking across ESMs. These emulators will support the creation of diagnostic uncertainty maps that pinpoint where model convergence conceals mechanistic divergence. For model development and parameter learning, I will design a coupled deep learning system that integrates feed-forward neural networks (NNs) and long short-term memory (LSTM) NNs. The LSTM will address temporal autocorrelation, particularly for carbon pool variables such as plant biomass and soil carbon. Importantly, model parameters will be included as features within the emulator, enabling it to learn parameter sensitivities and biases. A secondary neural network will perform statistical parameter learning, using experimental benchmarks as labels. This end-to-end, label-free training architecture allows the emulator to infer and correct model biases, thereby reducing uncertainty in carbon–climate feedback projections.

Application of ML to microbial biome analysis and ecosystem modeling

Machine learning is transforming microbial ecology by enabling the classification, prediction, and interpretation of complex, high-dimensional data. Classical algorithms such as random forests, support vector machines, and gradient boosting have long supported taxonomic classification and biomarker discovery. More recently, deep learning architectures—including convolutional neural networks (CNNs), recurrent neural networks (RNNs), and transformer-based embeddings—have enhanced our ability to model microbial temporal dynamics and integrate multi-omics data. These tools not only improve predictive accuracy but also enable discovery of novel ecological patterns that traditional statistical approaches cannot easily capture. To enhance interpretability, my group will employ explainable AI methods such as SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) and Locally Interpretable Model-Agnostic Explanations (LIME) to ensure that predictions highlight ecologically meaningful drivers such as microbial interactions, keystone taxa, or functional guilds. Leveraging one of the longest-running field experiments at OU’s Kessler Farm (Figure 2), where temperature, precipitation, and harvest treatments have been continuously manipulated since 2009, we will integrate microbial dynamics into ecosystem carbon models using ML. The central hypothesis is that microbial temporal dynamics encode information about functional traits (e.g., carbon use efficiency, mortality rate) relevant to soil carbon cycling. Through this approach, we aim to bridge microbial ecology and ecosystem carbon cycle, offering new predictive capacity for global carbon–climate feedback. Together, these advances demonstrate how ML methods are not only improving our ability to classify and predict microbial community dynamics but also reshaping the way microbial ecology interfaces with ecosystem and climate research. These efforts will establish a data–model integration hub at OU, bridging biology, statistics, and Earth system science. I envision close collaboration with the School of Biological Sciences, the Institute for Environmental Genomics, and national laboratory partners to co-develop open-source algorithms for biological model calibration, benchmarking, and ML integration. My long-term vision is to contribute to OU’s growing strength in quantitative biology by fostering collaborations where biological statistics connect data science, ecology, and climate modeling to better understand Earth’s dynamic biosphere.
